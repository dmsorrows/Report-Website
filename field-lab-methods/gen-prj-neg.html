Generic Project (Negative Findings)

 

Option 1. Polygon(s)

 

INVESTIGATION METHODS AND RESULTS

 

The archeological fieldwork was directed by archeologist Chris Branam, RPA. He was assisted in the field by archeologist Lyndsay Ballew, MSc, Brandon Tully, BA, Ryan Adams, BA, and Jake Madle, BA. Field work was conducted on October 26 through November 2, 2020. McGeorge Contracting Co., Inc. provided Flat Earth Archeology with two KMZs detailing the perimeters of the proposed Project Area prior to the commencement of fieldwork. Personnel utilized these devices to maintain real-time location data on handheld electronic mapping devices, allowing the archeologists to accurately progress along each prescribed transect within the proposed Project Area. Field data was recorded using custom fillable PDF forms on portable devices. The forms provided fields for transect, shovel test number, meters on the transect, notes, and result of the test.

 

Flat Earth Archeology defined the west polygon as Area A and the east polygon as Area B. Area A and Area B of proposed Project Area are comprised of approximately 22.1 ha (54.8 ac) (Figures 42 and 43). Personnel excavated a total of 448 shovel tests within the proposed Project Area, not including 178 shovel test locale that remained unexcavated. Flat Earth Archeology established 32 transects in Area A of the proposed Project Area, lettered A through FF. Twenty transects were established in Area B of the proposed Project Area, lettered A through T. Shovel tests were investigated at a maximum of 20-m intervals along each transect during the pedestrian survey.

 

Area A of the proposed Project Area is comprised of approximately 17 ha (42.1 ac). Flat Earth Archeology personnel excavated a total of 310 shovel tests within Area A, not including 140 shovel tests that remained unexcavated. Flat Earth Archeology established 32 transects over the survey area, lettered A through FF. Shovel tests were investigated at a maximum of 20-m intervals along each transect during the pedestrian survey. Transects progressed at 0° and 180° commencing and terminating at the northern and southern perimeters of the survey area. At its maximum extents, the survey area extended approximately 404 m x 540 m (1,325 ft x 1,772 ft).

 

Area B of the proposed Project Area is comprised of approximately 5.1 ha (12.7 ac). Flat Earth Archeology personnel excavated a total of 138 shovel tests within Area B, not including 38 shovel tests that remained unexcavated. Flat Earth Archeology established 20 transects over the survey area, lettered A through T. Shovel tests were investigated at a maximum of 20-m intervals along each transect during the pedestrian survey. Transects progressed at 0° and 180° commencing and terminating at the northern and southern perimeters of the survey area. At its maximum extents, the survey area extended approximately 224 m x 318 m (735 ft x 1,043 ft).

 

Ground surface visibility was poor, varying between 0 percent and 25 percent. Woodland understory and leaf litter hindered surface visibility. Representative views of the landscape within Area A of the proposed Project Area are shown in Figures 44 through 51. Representative views of the landscape within Area B of the proposed Project Area are shown in Figures 60 through 71. All the soils from excavated shovel tests were screened through ¼ inch hardware cloth. Shovel tests were a minimum of 30 centimeter (cm) in diameter. Additionally, subsurface investigations typically extended 50 cm below the ground surface, 20 cm into the subsoil, until such point as a determination could be made regarding the nature of the soils, or an obstruction prohibited further excavation.

 

The circumstances that disallowed for the excavation of the unexcavated shovel tests included shovel test in roadway with impenetrable gravel deposition, shovel test on slope exceeding 20 percent grade, shovel test in lake, shovel test in fully excavated borrow pit area, and shovel test in inaccessible water compound area. The soil stratigraphy was recorded for each shovel test using Munsell color charts and standard soil textural descriptions. Representative samples of shovel test profiles are shown in Figures 52 through 59 for Area A. Representative samples of shovel test profiles are shown in Figures 72 through 74 for Area B. A shovel test inventory for the project is listed in Appendix E. The above-ground survey and subsurface survey proved negative for cultural materials.

 

Option 2. Corridor with Defined Boundary

 

INVESTIGATION METHODS AND RESULTS

 

Chris Branam, RPA, served as the principal investigator for the project. The archeological fieldwork was conducted by archeologist Chris Branam. Field work was conducted on June 18th, 2021. GBMc & Associates provided Flat Earth Archeology with a map and KMZs detailing the perimeter of the Project Area prior to the commencement of fieldwork (Figure 23). Flat Earth Archeology utilized ArcMap 10.4 to establish one transect within the Project Area and created a polyline shapefile delineating the transect. Additionally, personnel established shovel test locales along the transect polyline shapefile at 20-m intervals. Shapefiles were created based upon the shovel test locale point data. Flat Earth Archeology converted these shapefiles to KMZs. The archeologist utilized these devices to maintain real-time location data on handheld electronic mapping devices, allowing the archeologist to accurately investigate each shovel test locale along the prescribed transect within the Project Area. Field data was recorded using custom fillable PDF forms on portable devices. The forms provided fields for transect, shovel test number, meters on the transect, notes, and result of the test. The Project Area are comprised of approximately 3.3 ha (8.16 ac). The transect for the Project Area extended approximately 6,012 m (19,723 ft). The archeologist excavated a total of 24 shovel tests within the Project Area, not including 193 shovel tests that remained unexcavated. The varying occurrences of the unexcavated shovel tests for this area included shovel test on impenetrable asphalt street, shovel test on 7th Street Bridge, etc.

 

Personnel progressed along the transect at variable directions commencing at the Tall Timber Boulevard. The archeologist continued the pedestrian survey eastward along the transect, investigating each shovel test locale. The pedestrian survey continued along the transect across Brodie Creek and roughly paralleling Fourche Creek and powerline corridor. Continuing to the north, the archeologist excavated shovel tests before encountering Falls Boulevard. The archeologist continued the pedestrian survey northward across Colonel Glenn Road, Rock Creek, and W 36th Street until terminating at a pedestrian trail in Boyle Park.

 

Ground surface visibility was poor, varying between 0 percent and 25 percent. Short grass and leaf litter hindered surface visibility. Representative views of the landscape of the Project Area are shown in Figures 18 through 28. All the soils from excavated shovel tests were screened through ¼ inch hardware cloth. Shovel tests were a minimum of 30 centimeter (cm) in diameter. Additionally, subsurface investigations typically extended 50 cm below the ground surface, 20 cm into the subsoil, until such point as a determination could be made regarding the nature of the soils, or an obstruction prohibited further excavation. Additionally, the archeologist excavated in 10 cm levels at each shovel test. The soil stratigraphy was recorded for each shovel test using Munsell color charts and standard soil textural descriptions. Representative samples of shovel test profiles are shown in Figures 29 through 35. The above-ground survey and subsurface survey proved negative for cultural materials.

 

Option 3. Corridor without Defined Boundary

 

INVESTIGATION METHODS AND RESULTS

 

Chris Branam, RPA acted as principal investigator for this project. The archeological fieldwork was conducted by archeologist Chris Branam. Field work was conducted on June 3, 2021. Prior to the commencement of fieldwork, Miller-Newell Engineers, Inc. provided Flat Earth Archeology with maps detailing the centerlines of the proposed waterline improvements comprising the Project Area (Figure 16). Flat Earth Archeology utilized ArcMap 10.4 to georeference the maps provided by Miller-Newell Engineers, Inc. and created polyline shapefiles along the proposed waterline improvement centerlines. These data were converted to KMZ files to facilitate the use on handheld electronic mapping devices. The archeologist utilized this device to maintain real-time location data, allowing the archeologist to accurately progress along each prescribed transect within the Project Area. Field data was recorded using custom fillable PDF forms on portable devices. The forms provided fields for transect, shovel test number, meters on the transect, notes, and result of the test.

 

Flat Earth Archeology divided the Project Area into nine linear transects, numbered 1 through 9. The nine linear transects measure a total of 3.6 km (2.2 mi) (Figure 17). Flat Earth Archeology established a 9.1 m (30 ft) wide corridor along the linear transects to adequately identify cultural resources within and proximal to the polylines detailing the centerlines of the proposed waterline improvements comprising the Project Area. Shovel tests were excavated at a maximum of 20 m intervals along each transect during the pedestrian survey.

 

Transect 1 contained 13 shovel tests, not including one shovel test that remained unexcavated. Beginning at the western perimeter of Transect 1, the pedestrian survey of Transect 1 proceeded east paralleling E 3rd Street, thence proceeded south paralleling Wood Street and terminated at the southern perimeter of Transect 1. Transect 2 contained 13 shovel tests, not including one shovel test that remained unexcavated. Beginning at the western perimeter of Transect 2, the pedestrian survey of Transect 2 proceeded east paralleling Cherry Street, thence proceeded north paralleling Ritter Street and terminated at the northern perimeter of Transect 2. Transect 3 contained 13 shovel tests, not including one shovel test that remained unexcavated. Beginning at the northern perimeter of Transect 3, the pedestrian survey of Transect 3 proceeded southwest paralleling N Madison Street, and terminated at the southern perimeter of Transect 3.

 

Ground surface visibility was poor, varying between 0 percent and 25 percent. Short grass hindered surface visibility. Representative views of the landscape of the Project Area are shown in Figures 18 through 28. All the soils from excavated shovel tests were screened through ¼ inch hardware cloth. Shovel tests were a minimum of 30 centimeter (cm) in diameter. Additionally, subsurface investigations typically extended 50 cm below the ground surface, 20 cm into the subsoil, until such point as a determination could be made regarding the nature of the soils, or an obstruction prohibited further excavation. Additionally, the archeologist excavated in 10 cm levels at each shovel test.

 

The circumstances that disallowed for the excavation of the unexcavated shovel tests included shovel test in area of severe mechanical disturbance and shovel test in active drainage. Personnel used standard soil textural definitions and Munsell color notation for soil stratigraphy descriptions for selected shovel tests. Representative samples of shovel test profiles are shown in Figures 29 through 35. A shovel test inventory for the project is listed in Appendix B. The above-ground survey and subsurface survey proved negative for cultural materials.
