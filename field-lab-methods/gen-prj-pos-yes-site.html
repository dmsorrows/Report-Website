Generic Project (Positive Findings with Site)

 

Option 1. Polygon(s)

 

LABORATORY METHODOLOGY

 

Artifacts recovered during this project were assigned Field Serial Numbers (FSN) denoting the minimum spatial provenience. The FSNs were assigned sequentially by 10-cm level in shovel tests. The FSN was determined by when the artifacts were recorded into the FSN log by the individual archeologists in the field. Surface finds received an FSN. Upon delivery of artifacts to the lab, Flat Earth Archeology personnel confirmed the contents of each artifact bag and logged it on the Lab Intake Ledger Form. Artifacts were verified against the FSN logs from the field.

 

Prior to analysis, the artifacts were processed according to established best practices for each material type. New lab tags were created to capture provenience written on any field bags and or tags and placed on the drying screens with the contents of each bag. Project, spatial, site, and general notes were transcribed onto the new archival quality tags. If appropriate, artifacts were gently washed in warm water with soft brushes and placed on screen racks to air dry. Alternative methods of cleaning involved gentle dry brushing with a soft bristled brush. Processing of artifacts was conducted either by Lab Manager Laura Fuentes, RPA or under their direct supervision. The artifacts were roughly sorted during processing based on material types such as stone, glass, ceramic, pottery, or metal. Once fully processed and dried, artifacts were transferred with their Lab Bag Tags to archival quality white block bags until they could be analyzed. In the event that materials were determined to be naturally occurring instead of cultural materials, they were noted as such in the FSN log and deaccessioned using an appropriate method.

 

All artifacts were sorted based on their primary material composition into basic Laboratory Serial Number (LSN) classes: Unmodified Raw Material, Stone, Fauna and Flora, Pottery, Ceramic, Glass, Metal, Other & Unknown. Pre and Post Contact artifacts were further defined based on observable characteristics, further refining the basic material classification (i.e.: Burlington Chert, Novaculite, Shell Tempered, Stoneware, Brown Glass...). This second category is defined as the first level of the Artifact Serial Number (ASN 1). The third division (ASN 2) describes use, function, category, or diagnostic form (i.e.: Tertiary Flake, Bowl Sherd, Cut Nail, Beer Can Pull Tab...). The fourth and final division in artifact sorting (ASN 3) is used to describe specific diagnostic decoration styles, treatments, shapes, or sizes (i.e.: Burnished, Heat Modified, Transferware, Embossed, 16-D (1'' nail size)).

 

This model of artifact analysis is built as an adaptation of both DELOS: A Computerized Artifact Inventory and Analysis System (Cande and Barnes 1992) and the Digital Archaeological Archive of Comparative Slavery's Cataloging Manual (DAACS 2018). Cande's work provided the base model for category divisions, moving from general material types to more specific observable characteristics and types. Specific definitions and type restrictions were used as a guideline for identifying artifact types or subsets. The Digital Archaeological Archive of Comparative Slavery's (DAACS) was referenced to help delineate specific categories or characteristic definitions and parameters.

 

The artifact catalog includes the complete catalog number for each artifact, comprised of the accession number assigned by the Arkansas Archeological Survey (ARAS), the FSN, LSN, ASN, ASN 1, and ASN 2 and ASN 3 as appropriate. Lab personnel counted, weighed, and described all unique or diagnostic characteristics of each artifact. A representative sample of artifact types and frequency were photographed using a color photo scale utilizing the metric scale. Additionally, diagnostic or unique artifacts were selected to be photographed. All artifacts were prepared for permanent curation in accordance with the most recently updated standards of the University of Arkansas Collections Facility in Fayetteville, Arkansas (University of Arkansas Collections Facility 2018).

 

Generalized Temporal Analysis

 

Determinations of cultural affiliations or periods in the site description was informed by the type, frequency, and spatial context (Pre-Contact or Post-Contact) of artifacts observed and narrowed by diagnostic artifacts when possible. Rough dates are often used for Post-Contact sites that contain artifacts with varying date ranges (e.g., an assemblage containing both nineteenth century slipped stoneware and twentieth century wire nails would be described as late-nineteenth to middle twentieth century). Specific Post-Contact artifact types can be used to date sites in reports such as stoneware, nails, bottles, jar glass, etc. For example, stoneware roughly dates from the middle nineteenth century through the 1930s. Stoneware vessel types were increasingly replaced by glass canning jars beginning after the first decade of the twentieth century. However, stoneware vessels were produced over a long period, were often long-lived, and passed through generations. It is important to consider this when considering the manufacture date (Stewart-Abernathy 1980, Stelle 2001). Likewise, the broad temporal expanse of earthenware production confounds efforts to establish firm dates. Stoneware and earthenware are common in the region of the Project Area and well-documented throughout nineteenth and twentieth century sites in Arkansas. Whiteware, non-vitreous white bodied earthenware, are generally almost pure white in color. Majewski and O’Brien (1987:119) argue that earthenware producers were probably by the popularity of bone china in the first quarter of the nineteenth century. By 1840 whiteware was established as a separate earthenware type and it is still produced today. Whiteware is decorated and an assortment of decorative techniques was used at various times throughout the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

 

Cut nails observed at some Post-Contact sites are attributed to the late nineteenth century and wire nails are mostly attributed to the twentieth century in Flat Earth Archeology's artifact analysis. Cut nails had become common enough to replace the widespread use of wrought nails during the nineteenth century (Edwards and Wells 1993:17). Cut nail manufacturing in the United States had been growing since the turn of the nineteenth century, increasing in accessibility and decreasing in price, making cut nails a favorable choice among builders. By 1900, wire nails made from Bessemer Steel largely replaced cut nails in the market (Edwards and Wells 1993:18).

 

Bottles and bottle fragments were examined for manufacturer’s marks and seam lines on the bottle lip and finish when present to determine date ranges for specific manufacturing technologies and trends the bottle falls within (e.g., an early machine-made bottle will have a single seam running over the entire finish). Screw top finishes are only present on bottles post-dating 1919 (Stelle 2001:13). For the purpose of this report, the generalized temporal analysis of the Post-Contact artifacts discussed above is not repeated in the Site Description. Any relevant recovered artifacts are depicted in the Site Description and the artifact analysis is summarized in tables, with associated provenience information. All manufacturer's marks present on artifacts were researched to obtain additional information. Any visible mark, complete or incomplete, is described in detail in the Site Description. Collections and analysis forms will be curated by the ARAS under the assigned permanent accession number 2022-424. Laura Fuentes conducted the artifact analysis.

 

INVESTIGATION METHODS AND RESULTS

 

The archeological fieldwork was directed by archeologist Chris Branam, RPA. He was assisted in the field by archeologist Lyndsay Ballew, MSc, Brandon Tully, BA, Ryan Adams, BA, and Jake Madle, BA. Field work was conducted on October 26 through November 2, 2020. McGeorge Contracting Co., Inc. provided Flat Earth Archeology with two KMZs detailing the perimeters of the proposed Project Area prior to the commencement of fieldwork. Personnel utilized these devices to maintain real-time location data on handheld electronic mapping devices, allowing the archeologists to accurately progress along each prescribed transect within the proposed Project Area. Field data was recorded using custom fillable PDF forms on portable devices. The forms provided fields for transect, shovel test number, meters on the transect, notes, and result of the test.

 

Flat Earth Archeology defined the west polygon as Area A and the east polygon as Area B. Area A and Area B of proposed Project Area are comprised of approximately 22.1 ha (54.8 ac) (Figures 42 and 43). Personnel excavated a total of 448 shovel tests within the proposed Project Area, not including 178 shovel test locale that remained unexcavated. Flat Earth Archeology established 32 transects in Area A of the proposed Project Area, lettered A through FF. Twenty transects were established in Area B of the proposed Project Area, lettered A through T. Shovel tests were investigated at a maximum of 20-m intervals along each transect during the pedestrian survey.

 

Area A of the proposed Project Area is comprised of approximately 17 ha (42.1 ac). Flat Earth Archeology personnel excavated a total of 310 shovel tests within Area A, not including 140 shovel tests that remained unexcavated. Flat Earth Archeology established 32 transects over the survey area, lettered A through FF. Shovel tests were investigated at a maximum of 20-m intervals along each transect during the pedestrian survey. Transects progressed at 0° and 180° commencing and terminating at the northern and southern perimeters of the survey area. At its maximum extents, the survey area extended approximately 404 m x 540 m (1,325 ft x 1,772 ft).

 

Area B of the proposed Project Area is comprised of approximately 5.1 ha (12.7 ac). Flat Earth Archeology personnel excavated a total of 138 shovel tests within Area B, not including 38 shovel tests that remained unexcavated. Flat Earth Archeology established 20 transects over the survey area, lettered A through T. Shovel tests were investigated at a maximum of 20-m intervals along each transect during the pedestrian survey. Transects progressed at 0° and 180° commencing and terminating at the northern and southern perimeters of the survey area. At its maximum extents, the survey area extended approximately 224 m x 318 m (735 ft x 1,043 ft).

 

Ground surface visibility was poor, varying between 0 percent and 25 percent. Woodland understory and leaf litter hindered surface visibility. Representative views of the landscape within Area A of the proposed Project Area are shown in Figures 44 through 51. Representative views of the landscape within Area B of the proposed Project Area are shown in Figures 60 through 71. All the soils from excavated shovel tests were screened through ¼ inch hardware cloth. Shovel tests were a minimum of 30 centimeter (cm) in diameter. Subsurface investigations typically extended 50 cm below the ground surface, 20 cm into the subsoil, until such point as a determination could be made regarding the nature of the soils, or an obstruction prohibited further excavation. Additionally, the archeologists excavated in 10 cm levels at each shovel test.

 

The circumstances that disallowed for the excavation of the unexcavated shovel tests included shovel test in roadway with impenetrable gravel deposition, shovel test on slope exceeding 20 percent grade, shovel test in lake, shovel test in fully excavated borrow pit area, and shovel test in inaccessible water compound area. The soil stratigraphy was recorded for each shovel test using Munsell color charts and standard soil textural descriptions. Representative samples of shovel test profiles are shown in Figures 52 through 59 for Area A. Representative samples of shovel test profiles are shown in Figures 72 through 74 for Area B. A shovel test inventory for the project is listed in Appendix E. The above-ground survey and subsurface survey produced positive results. Shovel Tests 2 and 3 of Transect B, Shovel Tests 1 and 2 of Transect C, and Shovel Test 1 of Transect D of Area B produced positive results. Site 3WA1582 is discussed separately below.
