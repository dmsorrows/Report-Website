Cell Tower (Positive Findings without Site)

 

Option 1. Monopole

 

INVESTIGATION METHODS AND RESULTS

 

The archeological fieldwork was conducted by archeologist Devin Sorrows, RPA. Field work was conducted on October 28, 2021. The archeologist spent roughly one hour in the field on this survey. Maps and coordinates of the Project Area were provided prior to fieldwork by EnviroWest (Figure 34). The proposed Project Area is located on a concrete sidewalk.

 

The small area of the proposed metal pole footprint defining the Project Area is less than one square meter and is completely covered by concrete. Therefore, the archeologist excavated one shovel test locale as proximal as possible to the Project Area. The shovel test locale was investigated roughly 4 meters to the west-northwest of the Project Area (Figure 35). All the soils from excavated shovel test locale were screened through ¼-inch hardware cloth. The shovel test locale was approximately 40 centimeter (cm) in diameter. The subsurface investigation extended at least 20 cm into the subsoil. Additionally, the shovel test locale was excavated in 10 cm levels. The soil stratigraphy was recorded for the shovel test using Munsell color charts and standard soil textural descriptions. The shovel test profile is shown in Figures 36. Table 2 contains a shovel test inventory for the project.

 

The ground surface of the Project Area was examined during the pedestrian survey. The Project Area was walked in roughly 5-m intervals, trending north/south beginning at the northwest corner of the Project Area. Ground surface visibility was poor, varying between 0 percent and 25 percent. Short grass and concrete hindered surface visibility. Representative views of the landscape within the Project Area are shown in Figures 37 through 41. The above-ground survey and subsurface survey produced modern cultural materials associated with the demolished historical structure situated near the southern perimeter of Area A. Two modern bottle glass shards and one modern plastic fragment were discovered in the A horizon of Shovel Test 3, Transect A. Two modern bottle glass shards were discovered in the A horizon of Shovel Test 1, Transect B. A modern above ground surface scatter consisting of glass bottle shards, rusted tin cans, beer cans, plastic fragments, and plastic bottles was observed near Shovel Tests 1 through 3 of Transect A through C. Additionally, fencing and a small patch of yucca was observed near Shovel Test 1, Transect A (Figures 75 through 78). These cultural materials are likely associated with the demolition event that occurred between February 2012 and November 2012 (Figures 37 and 38). These cultural materials do not meet the criteria of an archeological site as defined by Davis, ed. (1982, amended 2010:B-8). Therefore, the modern cultural materials were not recorded as an archeological site.

 

Option 2. Lease Area with Easement

 

INVESTIGATION METHODS AND RESULTS

 

The archeological fieldwork was directed by archeologist Chris Branam, RPA. He was assisted in the field by archeologist Ryan Adams, BA. Field work was conducted on August 23, 2019. The archeologists spent roughly four hours in the field on this survey. Maps and coordinates of the proposed Project Area were provided prior to fieldwork by Ramaker & Associates, Inc. (Figure 16). The proposed Project Area was staked in the field to demarcate the locations of the proposed Lease Area. The proposed Project Area is located on a grassy lawn.

 

A total of seven shovel tests locales were excavated within the proposed Project Area, not including three shovel test locales that remained unexcavated (Figure 17). The circumstances that disallowed for the excavation of the unexcavated locales included locale on roadway with impenetrable gravel deposition. One shovel test locale was excavated at the proposed tower center point. One shovel test locale was excavated in each corner of the 22.9 m x 22.9 m (.13 ac) proposed Lease Area. Four shovel test locales were excavated within the 9.1 m x 123 m (15 ft x 240 ft) proposed Access Easement. The survey of the proposed Access Easement consisted of a single transect progressing at 283° from the west perimeter of the proposed Lease Area and terminating at the west line of the property parcel. Shovel test locales were investigated at a maximum of 15-m intervals along the easement transect.

 

All the soils from excavated shovel test locales were screened through ¼-inch hardware cloth. Shovel tests were a minimum of 40 centimeter (cm) in diameter. Subsurface investigations typically extended 20 cm into the subsoil, until such point as a determination could be made regarding the nature of the soils, or an obstruction prohibited further excavation. Additionally, shovel test locales were excavated in 10 cm levels. The soil stratigraphy was recorded for each shovel test using Munsell color charts and standard soil textural descriptions. Representative samples of the shovel test profiles are shown in Figures 18 and 19. Table 1 contains a shovel test inventory for the project.

 

The ground surface of the proposed Project Area was examined during the pedestrian survey. The proposed Lease Parcel and Access Easement were walked in roughly 5-m intervals, trending north/south beginning at the northwest corner of the proposed Project Area. Ground surface visibility was poor, varying between 0 percent and 25 percent. Short grass hindered surface visibility. Representative views of the landscape within the proposed Project Area are shown in Figures 20 through 24. The above-ground survey and subsurface survey produced positive results.

 

Results Discussion

 

The above-ground survey and subsurface survey produced modern cultural materials associated with the demolished historical structure situated near the southern perimeter of Area A. Two modern bottle glass shards and one modern plastic fragment were discovered in the A horizon of Shovel Test 3, Transect A. Two modern bottle glass shards were discovered in the A horizon of Shovel Test 1, Transect B. A modern above ground surface scatter consisting of glass bottle shards, rusted tin cans, beer cans, plastic fragments, and plastic bottles was observed near Shovel Tests 1 through 3 of Transect A through C. Additionally, fencing and a small patch of yucca was observed near Shovel Test 1, Transect A (Figures 75 through 78). These cultural materials are likely associated with the demolition event that occurred between February 2012 and November 2012 (Figures 37 and 38). These cultural materials do not meet the criteria of an archeological site as defined by Davis, ed. (1982, amended 2010:B-8). Therefore, the modern cultural materials were not recorded as an archeological site.

 

The above-ground survey produced a concrete foundation and cultural materials associated with a demolished modern structure situated near Shovel Test 4, Transect EE of Area A. An above ground surface scatter consisting of multiple metal and plastic fragments cans were observed near the concrete foundation (Figures 79 through 81). These cultural materials and foundation are likely associated with a structure built between 1992 and February 1994 (Figures 83 and 83). The demolition of this structure occurred between February 1994 and April 2000 (Figure 84). The subsurface survey near the modern cultural materials and concrete foundation produced negative results. These modern cultural materials and concrete foundation do not meet the criteria of an archeological site as defined by Davis, ed. (1982, amended 2010:B-8). Therefore, they were not recorded as an archeological site.

 

The above-ground survey and subsurface survey produced modern cultural materials associated with the demolished historical structure situated near the southern perimeter of Area B. Seven modern bottle glass shards were discovered in the A horizon of Shovel Test 1, Transect J. A modern above ground surface scatter consisting of multiple corrugated metal cans, beer cans, large architectural debris and three paint cans were observed near the southmost extents of Transect J through M (Figures 85 and 86). These cultural materials are likely associated with the demolition event that occurred between 1957 and 1970 (Figures 39 and 40). Further disturbance likely occurred during the excavation of the proximal borrow pit area to the north between October 2017 and October 2018. These cultural materials do not meet the criteria of an archeological site as defined by Davis, ed. (1982, amended 2010:B-8). Therefore, the modern cultural materials were not recorded as an archeological site.
