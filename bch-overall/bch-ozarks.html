BRIEF CULTURAL HISTORY

 

Paleoindian period (12000-10500 B.P.)

 

The Paleo Indian period is considered the earliest confirmed period of human occupation in the state. Distinctive artifacts include fluted and unfluted lanceolate points and a variety of drills, gravers, burins, knives, and scrapers some of which carry over into the following Dalton period. Most Paleo Indian artifacts reported for the region in Arkansas have been isolated surface finds and no intact deposits or assemblages have been excavated or securely dated. Points are known from both bluff shelters and open sites, especially along river terraces and older elevated surfaces (Branam and Spears 2005:4)

 

Regional subsistence data is lacking, but based primarily on information obtained outside the state, Paleo Indian cultures were thought to have lived in nomadic groups and subsisted primarily by hunting animals and gathering wild plant foods (Branam and Spears 2005:4)

 

Dalton period (10500-9500 B.P.)

 

Dalton artifact assemblages are relatively common throughout the state and have been fairly well documented. The Dalton point is the most diagnostic artifact for this period but a number of other tools including adzes, spokeshaves, and abraders are also known. Carryovers from the preceding Paleo Indian period include side and end scrapers, gravers, burins, and perforators. A number of Dalton components have been excavated in good stratigraphic context in the Ozarks and radiocarbon dates have bracketed the period between 10,500-9,500 B.P. (Miller 2003). Dalton settlement patterns seem to be oriented along major stream valleys but sites in the Ozarks have been found in a wide variety of topographic situations ranging from bluff shelters to open terrace, bench, and hilltop locations. Dalton social organization is believed to have been a continuation of Paleo Indian traditions (Miller 2003).

 

Archaic period (9500-2500 B.P.)

 

The Archaic period marks a technological expansion reflected in the archeological record by an increasing diversity of stone tools. Wide varieties of stemmed and notched points appear as well as full grooved axes and celts. Archaic cultures employed many tools such as grinding stones, abraders, and pitted cobbles from the preceding periods. Twined fabrics first appear in the archeological record during this period, likely used to make sandals, bags, and other items.

 

Lithic Assemblage

The Rice Complex characterizes the Early Archaic period in the White River drainage of Northwest Arkansas. Diagnostic points include a variety of lanceolate, stemmed, and notched points as well as reworked, stemmed points and pitted anvil stones (Cande 2008). Examples of hafted, Rice points and lanceolate points, as well as unhafted bifaces and various scrapers occur in Early Archaic levels at the Albertson site in Benton County (Dickson 1991).

 

A Middle Archaic White River Complex has been identified for the White River Valley, characterized by several point styles, including Big Sandy, Jakie Stemmed, Rice Lobed, and Stone Square Stemmed. Other artifacts characteristic of the complex includes scrapers, expanding base drills, chipped, double bitted axes, and a variety of ground stone tools including celts, full grooved axes, pitted anvil stones, mortars, mullers, and hammerstones (Cande 2008; Dickson 1991; both after Chapman and Henning 1975). Type sites for this complex are Turner Cave in Madison County and several sites in the Beaver Reservoir (Scholtz 1967). There are elements of the White River Complex at numerous sites in Washington County, including Mitchell, the Tyson site, Winn Creek, and Wet Spot, as well as the Elk Track in the Upper Buffalo River Valley. The Mitchell site located near an outcrop of the Boone Formation, on the White River, appears to be a production site for one of the predominant projectile point styles of the complex. At this site, 27 of 31 Middle Archaic projectile points recovered were of the Big Sandy type, and the majority was made of local, white, Keokuk chert (Lafferty et al. 1988a).

 

A different cultural tradition, the Middle Archaic Tom’s Brook complex, occurred in the Arkansas River Valley and Ouachita Mountain region. Projectile points characteristic of this tradition are Big Sandy and Johnson (Cande 2008 after Wyckoff 1984). In Northwest Arkansas, Mitchell and the Tyson sites contain both point styles.

 

Other varieties of basal-notched, corner notched, square-stemmed, straight-stemmed or contracting-stemmed points occur at Middle Archaic sites in Northwest Arkansas. These include such diagnostic points as, Stone Square-Stemmed, Table Rock Stemmed, and White River Corner Notched (Beckman 1969).

 

Corner notched and contracting stem projectile points dominate Late Archaic period archeological sites in Arkansas. Common varieties of Late Archaic corner notched points include Williams, Big Creek, Kings Corner Notched, and Marcos. The most common contracting stem points for the period are Gary and Langtry. Chapman and Henning (1975) defined a Late Archaic James River Complex for the White River drainage based on type sites in the Table Rock Lake area of Southwest Missouri. Diagnostic points include Table Rock Stemmed, Afton Corner Notched, Stone Square Stemmed, and Smith Basal Notched. Some Northwest Arkansas archeological sites exhibit elements of this complex. Table Rock Stemmed points are found at the Albertson site and Elk Track for example (Dickson 1991, Lafferty et al. 1988b), and both Stone Square Stemmed and Afton points are found at the Mitchell site (Lafferty et al. 1988a).

 

The Wister phase is another Late Archaic cultural complex, represented at sites in the Oklahoma Ouachitas and the Arkansas River Valley. Diagnostic points of this complex include Gary, Marshall, Marcos, and Williams. Cutting tools, scrapers, ground stone tools, and bone and shell objects are also part of Wister phase assemblages (Cande 2008). Similarities with the Wister phase are apparent at several sites in Northwest Arkansas. For example, the Mitchell site produced all four point types diagnostic of the Wister phase, and Elk Track contains Wister elements, but lacks Williams points (Lafferty et al. 1988a, 1988b). Williams points were present at other sites such as Winn Creek, Wet Spot, and Cato Springs; Wet Spot also yielded Marcos points (Klinger et al. 1993, Lafferty et al. 1997).

 

Other common Late Archaic projectile point styles in Northwest Arkansas include Stone Corner Notched, Pandale, and Uvalde (Cande 2008). Stone Corner Notched and Uvalde points are found at Winn Creek (Klinger et al. 1993).

Fire Cracked Rock Features

FCR features are common from the Early Archaic through Middle Woodland periods (Lafferty et al. 1997). The form of these features varies from compact concentrations of thermally altered sandstone to very large (>60 m2), diffuse scatters of FCR. Some FCR concentrations are located within pits, such as at the Webb Branch site in Newton County and the Cato Springs site in Washington County (Lafferty et al. 1988b, 1997). FCR features at these two sites are associated with wood charcoal and charred hickory nut and walnut shells, suggesting these rock-lined pits served as locales for nut roasting (Lafferty et al. 1997). At other sites, bone is among the FCR, suggesting faunal food preparation. FCR features are especially common at open sites (e.g. Klinger et. al 1993, Lafferty et al. 1988a, Lafferty et al. 1988b, Lafferty et al. 1997), but have also been found in bluff shelters such as Brown Bluff in Washington County (Guendling and Renfro 2008a, Guendling and Renfro 2008). There are similar features at sites in Tennessee, Texas, and Oklahoma. Although very common throughout the Early Archaic and into the Woodland period, FCR features appear to go out of use around A.D. 1000, probably reflecting a shift in subsistence (Lafferty et al. 1997; 2003).

 

Subsistence

Many well-documented Archaic sites produced evidence of a wide variety of food resources. During the Early and Middle Archaic, smaller mammals especially squirrel and rabbit seem to increase in dietary importance, and this is likely associated with climatic and ecological changes that are known to have occurred between about 8000 to 4000 B. P. During this time, at open sites in Northwest Arkansas such as Elk Track, and bluff shelters such as Brown Bluff and the Albertson site, residents exploited a range of local food resources. Faunal remains at these sites include turkey, various small vertebrates, such as box turtle and fish, and shellfish, such as mussels (Lafferty et al. 1988b, Guendling 2008, Dickson 1991). There is also an increase in the number of types of artifacts generally associated with plant food processing at many Archaic period sites. A variety of mullers, millstones, digging tools, and possible floral shredders and pulpers, have been found at both open sites and bluff shelters (Dickson 1991). Archeologists recovered remains of cultivated native plants such as chenopodeum, sunflower, knotweed, maygrass, and sumpweed from shelters (Abbott 2008, Guendling and Renfro 2008b). By the latter part of the Archaic, domestication of plants-notably squash and the bottle gourd-is evident at some Archaic sites (e.g. Cato Springs and Wet Spot: Lafferty et al. 1997). As cultigens became more important, diversity of plant food resources may have decreased. In the stratified deposits of Brown Bluff, for example, Abbott (2008:253-554) noted a greater variety of seed types in Archaic and Woodland middens than in later deposits. He proposed that the lack of diversity in more recent deposits might reflect an increasing reliance on maize agriculture during the Mississippi period, with less emphasis on local seasonal resources, although Guendling and Renfro (2008b) cautioned that food remains at bluff shelters do not represent an entire subsistence strategy.

 

Settlement and Site Specialization

Settlement patterns are primarily oriented toward river valley settings but sites occur in just about every topographic situation available and seem to reflect a wide variety of activities centered on seasonal exploitation of the environment. By the end of the Archaic period, it is clear that some base-camp sites are becoming relatively permanent year-round habitations (Miller 2003). Dickson (1991) suggests that the Spavinaw site, located on Spavinaw Creek in Benton County, may have been a semi-permanent base camp. The Lake Sequoyah site (Beckman 1969) is a possible occupational site in Washington County. Other large camps suggest permanent settlement was occurring near major rivers. Many other sites in riverine environments may have eroded or buried under very deep alluvium (Dickson 1991, Lafferty et al. 1997). While evidence is still lacking to indicate what sorts of structures Archaic period people occupied, the presence of hearths and pits suggest more than temporary occupation at many sites.

 

Site specialization is evident during the Late Archaic period. Bluff shelters, such as Rodgers (Kay 1982), Albertson (Dickson 1991), and Brown Bluff (Guendling 2008), appear to have been used as seasonal hunting and/or food processing sites. Developmental age of deer remains at Brown Bluff suggests an early spring occupation of the site (Hilliard 2008). Alternately, at Albertson, deer development suggests summer through winter occupation. Many open sites such as Cato Springs, Wet Spot, Graham Farm, and Winn Creek in Washington County also represent short-term camps for hunting and associated activities (Lafferty et al. 1997). Fall and winter occupation is evident at Cato Springs and Wet Spot because of botanical remains found at the sites, including acorns and nut hulls. In addition to hunting, nut processing appears to be an important function at least at Cato Springs, and evidence of primary tool manufacture is apparent at Graham Farm.

 

In contrast to the hunting camps mentioned above, weapons represent the smallest artifact class at the Mitchell site (Lafferty et al. 1988a). Located near a chert outcrop of the Boone formation on the White River, the predominant function of this site is lithic procurement and primary reduction. Cores and bifaces are the most abundant artifact class. Lafferty et al. (1988a) suggested that primary reduction took place at Mitchell, with preforms transported to upland sites. Other tool classes including, woodworking, utility, and domestic, also came from the site. Types of activities evident at the site are generally consistent through the Late Archaic with the predominance of stoneworking somewhat decreasing over time.

 

Digging tools, associated with harvesting plant foods, suggest summer use of the Mitchell site. The presence of wild foods available only during autumn, including wild grape pips and hickory and Juglandaceae (Walnut family) nutshell fragments, indicate occupation during the fall. The site produced abundant wood charcoal, with very few associated botanical remains, which may indicate wood use for heating as opposed to cooking. This is also consistent with utilization of the site during colder months.

 

Generalized activities at Elk Track, an open site on the Buffalo River, include stone working tools and weaponry, represented in equal numbers, as well as domestic artifacts, general utility items, and woodworking tools (Lafferty et al.1988b). A decrease in weapons and stone working tools into the Late Archaic exemplify a trend toward less generalization at the site. Because of the diversity of artifacts, this site is likely a habitation site that would have included men, women, and children. Although it was likely occupied by family groups rather than male hunting parties, Elk Track still seems to be a temporary seasonal camp. Wild food remains of hickory nuts, acorns, persimmon seeds, sumac seeds, and wild grape pips suggest late summer and fall seasonality (Lafferty et al. 1988b).

 

Lafferty et al. (1997) suggest a seasonal round for the White River area during the Archaic period where summers were spent at lowland sites such as Mitchell, and then transient camps, such as Cato Springs and Wet Spot were utilized during fall and winter, as inhabitants trekked toward the Boston Mountain uplands or the Arkansas River Valley where water was readily available during the winter. In fact, both Cato Springs and Wet Spot contain tools made of chert from bottomland sources (Lafferty et al. 1997). The Tyson site, in Washington County near Fayetteville, has an artifact assemblage similar to that of these two sites, suggesting it was also a transient camp. However, the site appears to be part of a different seasonal round. It lacks evidence of nut processing or FCR features, and tools are primarily made with local cherts and gravels instead of Keokuk chert (from the Mitchell site).

 

Trade and Travel

Throughout much of the southeast, many Archaic period cultures were part of elaborate trade networks. However, aside from the bottle gourd and squash (both Mexican cultigens), there is little evidence of trade during the Archaic period in the Ozark Mountain region. Although evidence of extensive multi-regional trade is lacking, artifacts made of exotic material at a few sites may suggest at least regional trade or travel. Brown Bluff, Winn Creek, and Graham Farm, all located in Washington County, produced tools made of Novaculite, which originates south of the Arkansas River in the Ouachita Mountains (Guendling and Renfro 2008a, Guendling and Renfro 2008, Klinger et al. 1993). Winn Creek additionally yielded artifacts made of Permian Florence Chert Variety 2, which has sources in Cowley County, Kansas and Kay County, Oklahoma. Graham Farm also produced artifacts made of Tahlequah chert from Ottawa County, Oklahoma and Wreford Chert from Cowley County, Kansas (Klinger et al. 1993).

 

Cultural Conservatism

Late Archaic period cultural traditions persist into the Woodland period in the Ozarks. Lafferty et al. (1988b) have suggested conservation of culture for the region, citing the lack of ceramics at Ozark sites until the Middle Woodland. Radiometric dating at the Brady site, located on the upper White River near Fayetteville, supports this claim. The artifact assemblage of this site is typical of a terminal Archaic period occupation; however, 14C dates suggest an age of only about 560 B.P. (+/- 110), generally considered Middle Woodland. (Scholtz n.d.).

 

Woodland period (2500-1100 B.P.)

 

Early Woodland period settlement patterns and lithic assemblages reflect a continuation of life ways and traditions developed during the preceding Archaic period. Plain grog or grit-tempered pottery appears for the first time and is generally considered a prime diagnostic of the period. Minor amounts of decorated pottery types (primarily stamped designs) have been found at some sites (Miller 2003). Other distinctive Woodland period artifacts include chipped silt stone spades or axes and Gary points. Toward the latter part of the period the bow and arrow appear and replaces the atlatl propelled dart as a hunting weapon (Branam and Spears 2005:7).

 

Woodland components in the region have been found in a variety of settings including bluff shelters and open locations. Many of the components appear to be seasonal occupations and do not reflect the types of activity (house building, mound building, burial ceremonialism, trade, etc.) found at many of the sites situated along major stream systems in adjacent regions (Branam and Spears 2005:7).

 

 

Mississippi period (1100-500 B.P.)

 

Based mainly from data obtained outside the Ozark Mountain region, Mississippi period occupation is usually characterized by villages with palisades, temple mounds, shell tempered pottery, heavy reliance on agriculture, and complex social systems. Compared with other regions, relatively little is known about the Mississippi period in the Ozarks. In the western part of the region, archeologists have identified two Mississippi period complexes. One of these is thought to be associated with the so called "Northern Caddo" occupation centered farther to the south in the Arkansas River valley while the other (the Neosho phase) is thought to represent the remains of the indigenous Osage (Miller 2003). Very few Mississippi period sites along the eastern Ozark escarpment have been extensively studied. Many of those dating to the early to middle Mississippi periods are probably closely related to the Gibson or Powers phase occupations in the adjacent Western lowlands. The Greenbrier phase is the only Late Mississippi period occupation defined in this portion of the region (Branam and Scoggin 2010:12).

 

Several mound sites have been identified in the Ozarks and most sites are situated in alluvial valley settings along major streams, however bluff shelters and upland sites are also known. Many of the latter appear to represent specialized or seasonal extractive camps (Branam and Scoggin 2010:12).

 

Flat-based shell tempered jar and bowl forms reflecting earlier Woodland period shapes have been found throughout much of the Ozark Mountain region. These appear to be indicators of Early Mississippi period occupation but some have strap handles and may date to the Middle Mississippi period. Late Mississippi period artifacts from the region include shell-tempered, incised and punctuated pottery, Nodena points and end scrapers (Branam and Scoggin 2010:13).

 

Proto Historic/Historic Indian occupation (500 – 200 B.P.)

 

Sites dating to this period in the Ozarks are rare and have been hard to identify. Historically much of the Ozarks was used for hunting and foraging and relatively little may exist in the archeological record. During the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, the Osage are known to have lived in several villages in what is now Missouri and northern Arkansas. Most of their hunting camps were probably used on a seasonal basis (Miller 2003). Further south, Cherokee migrant settlements have been excavated, however they are extremely hard to recognize based solely on material culture as they contain artifact assemblages nearly identical to other early-American sites of the same period (Miller 2003).
