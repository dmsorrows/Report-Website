BRIEF CULTURAL HISTORY

 

The general sequence of prehistoric cultural development in northeastern Arkansas is similar to that defined for the region as a whole and to the stages of cultural development in the southeastern United States. The major cultural stages are Paleo-Indian, Archaic, Woodland, Mississippi, Protohistoric, and Historic. In terms of European contact, the Nodena and Parkin people were the last recorded occupants of the northeastern Arkansas area. Following Hernando de Soto’s expedition during the summer of 1541, Native American populations seemed to nearly vanish. Furthermore, the expedition of Louis Jolliet and Jacques Marquette in 1673 along the Mississippi River saw almost no one in northeastern Arkansas, an area that which was once populated by the thousands. However, the first large villages Jolliet and Marquette discovered near the mouth of the Arkansas River were occupied by the “Akansea”, thought to be the ancestors of the modern Quapaw (Schambach and Newell 1990; 41). The culture history of this region has been previously summarized (House 1982; Klinger 1979; Rolingson 1972; Rolingson and Schambach 1981; Hemmings 1982; Goodyear 1982; McNutt 1996; O’Brien and Dunnell 1998; Smith 2007; Whayne et al. 2013) and need not be repeated in detail here. The following is a brief cultural history, or overview.

 

Little is known about the lifeways of the earliest inhabitants of the region during the Pre-projectile and Paleo-Indian stages (30,000 B.C. to 9,000 B.C.). Lithic or stone tools including large lanceolate-shaped fluted dart and spear points are distinctive markers of this period. Based on ethnographic studies of small-scale societies and paleo-environmental reconstruction, it is hypothesized that the small band level societies depended heavily on hunting, fishing, and gathering wild plants for subsistence. Many of the large late-Pleistocene species exploited during this period, such as the mammoth, are extinct. Most of the Paleo-Indian sites found in northeast Arkansas occur on the upper Cache River and on the eastern flank of Crowley’s Ridge (Morse and Morse 1983). The fluted Clovis-like points from northeast Arkansas are thought to be “typically shorter” than those found on the Great Plains. These surface finds have been comparatively dated with sites outside the region to about 9,000 to 10,000 B.C.

 

During the time-period of 11,000 to 10,000 B.P. (9000-8000 B.C.), the eastern United States experienced a climatic amelioration from more frigid, boreal-like forests to warmer, thermophilous deciduous forests. This gradual, environmental progression to a more humid climate prompted changes in various subsistence patterns in the Southeast. The Dalton Culture, which is defined as the transitional phase between the Late Paleo-Indian and Early Archaic projectile point traditions, is argued to have occurred between approximately 10,500 and 9900 B.P. or 8500 to 7900 B.C. (Goodyear 1982). The justification for this chronological placement is supported by Goodyear’s review of excavations performed in open-area, as opposed to sites found in caves and bluff-shelters in northeastern Arkansas. Goodyear concludes that basic stratigraphic and typological separateness defines the chronology for Dalton assemblages quite well. In that, various Dalton assemblages were discovered in strata altogether separate from adjacent, later occurring strata that included notched-point complexes. The utilization of a bluff shelters’ restricted space in upland environments in the Southeast United States combined with their frequent, long periods of use complicate the accurate interpretation of stratigraphy. Therefore, the utilization of these shelters are not, in most cases, suitable contexts for demonstrations of contemporaneity (Walthall 1998). Although these shelters present many difficulties in diachronic interpretation, it is evident that Dalton people were among the first to occupy caves and bluff shelters (Goodyear 1982).

 

The highly mobile foraging subsistence patterns that were characteristic of the those who lived during the Paleo-Indian stage are also characteristic those who lived during the Dalton phase. For example, the raw material that was utilized for portable, chipped lithic technologies suggests selection patterns designed to be anticipatory and flexible. The chipped stone tools used by Dalton foragers were typically manufactured on cryptocrystalline silicates. These manufacturing and selecting methods mimic the practices used by earlier Paleo-Indian peoples of North America. (Goodyear 1982).

 

Much of what is known about the Dalton period in Arkansas comes from the numerous Dalton period sites in northern Arkansas. Moreover, there have been over 750 Dalton period sites recorded in northeastern Arkansas alone (Coleman et al. 2013). Sites such as the Brand site, the Sloan site, and the Hawkins Cache in northeastern Arkansas have yielded a substantial amount of data about the Dalton period. All of the archeological investigations conducted on the aforementioned sites demonstrated that only Dalton points were functionally associated with Dalton components. In that, the points were discovered within a purely Dalton phase stratum among contemporaneous Dalton assemblages. The Brand Site in Poinsett County yielded 305 Dalton points and fragments, as well as five Early Archaic points and fragments. However, the non-Dalton, Early Archaic points and fragments were interpreted as the result of infrequent return visits to the site (Goodyear 1982). The investigation at the Sloan site in Greene County yielded 448 Dalton period artifacts including 144 Dalton points. Among the number of artifacts discovered, roughly 20 to 35 groups of artifacts were found spatially distributed in caches. Dan F. Morse, whose preliminary report details the excavations at the Sloan site, interprets the caches to be graves. As reflected at the Brand Site, three early Archaic points were recovered during the investigation. Again, these non-Dalton, Early Archaic points were interpreted as the result of a return visit to the site dated between 5,000 and 6,000 B.C. (Goodyear 1982).

 

Toward the end of the Paleo-Indian stage or the beginning of the Archaic period (9,000 B.C. to about 500 B.C.), the environment became much like it is today. In addition to Dalton projectile points, Packard projectile points dating to this transition are characterized by their lanceolate forms. These types are represented in this region and westward to the prairie-woodlands border in eastern Oklahoma (Wyckoff 1984:130-134). During the Archaic period people exploited a wider range of plant and animal resources than their Paleo-Indian ancestors. The subsistence base is reflected in the numerous types of projectile point styles and a wide range of tools found in the Archaic Period. In northeast Arkansas, the Hardin and Early Stemmed Period is associated with an influx of Plains-like styles, and dates about 7000-6000 B.C. (Morse and Morse 1983). Hardin points are rare, and again, have a distribution similar to Dalton points. Other stemmed points include Searcy, Johnson and Hidden Valley points (Morse and Morse 1983). The distribution of Johnson points extends to the southern portion of the Lower White River (House 1982). Later point types include Rice points (Morse and Morse 1983). Dalton culture artifacts are relatively common throughout the entire state and assemblages have been well defined at several sites outside the region.

 

During the Early and Middle Archaic periods, smaller mammals, especially squirrel and rabbit, seem to increase in dietary importance. This increase is likely a reflection of climatic and ecological changes that occurred throughout the Hypsithermal (6000 - 3000 B.C.). There is also a marked increase in the quantity of artifacts associated with plant food processing. By the latter part of this period, the domestication of plants, notably squash and the bottle gourd, is evident in western Arkansas. However, in contrast to the Dalton phase, evidence for Archaic sites in the northeastern region of Arkansas tend to be more scarce in the archeological record from about 8000-3000 B.C. Morse and Morse offer three likely explanations for the low frequency of observed material cultural within this region. Efforts by archeologists to accurately identify lithic typologies between the period of 6000-4000 B.C. may hinder our understanding of the Archaic period in this region. The scarcity of identified points in this region during the periods of 8000-6000 B.C. and 4000-3000 B.C. may also hamper our ability to accurately fill in the archeological record. Another possibility is that prehistoric occupants may have simply abandoned portions of the lowlands in search of permanent settlements. Lastly, population density may have been low in any given area in the region during this time period. If populations were distributed throughout the region, this may explain the increased difficulty of discovering intact archeological deposits in Northeastern Arkansas (McNutt 1996: 124).

 

Nevertheless, the archeological record reflects a resurgence in Late Archaic components in Northeastern Arkansas. The Late Archaic began at the end of the Hypsithermal period when the modern climate and ecosystems developed. There was a dramatic increase in the number of sites in the region, and for this reason Morse and Morse called this period the “Archaic Expansion.” During the Late Archaic, there was increased human adaptation to riverine environments and the culture appears to have been more sedentary than previous periods. In northeast Arkansas, the Late Archaic is subdivided into three sub periods: Big Creek (3000-2000 B.C.), Burkett (2000-1000 B.C.) and Weems (1000-500 B.C.). The Calion phase is the only Late Archaic phase defined for the region and includes Poverty Point period components typically marked by the presence of baked clay balls called “Poverty Point objects”, steatite vessel fragments, magnetite plummets and waste material, novaculite chipping debris, and Gary var. Gary points (Morse and Morse 1983).

 

The Woodland period (500 B.C. to A.D. 1000) is identified by the presence of pottery, and in some areas the beginning of horticulture and agriculture. The noticeable correlation of agriculture, pottery, and burial mounds suggests an increase in social and ceremonial activities during the Woodland period. Siltstone hoes, indicative of horticulture/agriculture, are associated with this period. The most commonly occurring diagnostic artifacts for the period are the Big Creek, Burkett, and Weems points and are likely carryovers from the preceding Archaic period. Other artifacts include adzes, celts, grooved axes, plummets, and choppers (Morse and Morse 1983). The development of ceramic vessels appeared during this period, indicating a greater reliance on food storage and preparation as well as more sedentary lifestyles. Early Woodland period settlement patterns and artifact assemblages throughout much of the southeast clearly reflect a continuation of life ways and traditions developed during the preceding Archaic period. There is undisputed evidence of house building, mound building, and elaborate burial ceremonialism throughout much of the southeast. The atlatl, which was extensively used as a means of hunting fauna during the Paleoindian and Archaic periods, is largely replaced by the bow and arrow in the Late Woodland period and into the Mississippi period. Pottery manufacture began and developed during the Woodland and is generally considered a prime diagnostic for the period (Schambach and Newell 1990; 34).

 

The regional sequence during the Woodland period has been subdivided into three periods: the Marksville period, the Tchula period, and the Baytown period. The Turkey Ridge and Pascola phases are subdivisions of the Early Woodland/Tchula period in Northeastern Arkansas, spanning the period from 500 B.C. to the beginning of the new millennium. Selected horizon markers for the Turkey Ridge and Pascola phases include biconical pottery objects, tetrapods on pot bases, Cormorant Cord impressed ceramics, Tchefuncte Stamped ceramics, Weems points, and reel-shaped gorgets (McNutt 1996: 131). Much of what is known about this period is illuminated by the McCarty site in Pope County. Although the McCarty site lies slightly west of the northeastern Arkansas region, the data acquired during the archeological investigations were enough to conclude that northeastern Arkansas occupants were mirroring the settlement patterns in the surrounding region (McNutt 1996: 125).

 

From turn of the millennium to A.D. 400, during the Middle Woodland/Marksville period, northeastern Arkansas is characterized by two distinctive cultural phases. Selected horizon markers for the Helena, Cow Mound, and Keller phases include exaggerated reel-shaped gorgets, Twin Lakes-like punctuated ceramics, vessels possessing Hopewell rims & related designs, fabric-impressed pottery, pottery botanicals, Hopewell designs, and Plain ceramics (McNutt 1996: 130-131). Late Woodland/Baytown period (A.D. 400-700) sites in northeastern Arkansas are categorized into the Hoecake, Baytown, and Dunklin phases. Selected horizon markers for these phases include mostly cord-marked pottery to the north, Baytown Ceramics, Barnes Ceramics, Steuben points, crude stemmed points, small chipped adzes, and Yates Net Impressed ceramics (McNutt 1996: 130). Assemblages from Late Woodland period sites in northeastern Arkansas indicate a large population within the region. However, Middle Woodland period sites discovered thus far, do not show evidence of condensed population centers other than the Mound City group near West Memphis. Further investigations in northeastern Arkansas may resolve the issue of unaccounted Middle Woodland populations in the area (McNutt 1996: 126).

 

By A.D. 900 there was an increased reliance on domesticated plants and a more complex social organization as evidenced by status differentiation, specialized labor, etc. These changes mark the beginning of the Mississippian period. Material culture includes arrow points and shell tempered pottery. This tempering technique allowed for the manufacture of a wider variety of vessel forms and decorations. Exotic materials and artistic artifacts, often decorated with religious and ritual symbols, were traded throughout the region. After death, important or high status individuals were interred with their possessions in sacred burial sites. Other characteristics often associated with Mississippian culture include villages with palisades, temple mounds, an increasing dependence on agriculture, and the development of complex social systems. Innovation of the bow and arrow occurred during the early part of the period and largely replaces the atlatl and dart common to the preceding periods. Pottery evolves from jar and bowl forms common in Early Period Mississippian sites to a variety of compound effigy and bottle forms characteristic of Late Period Mississippian and Protohistoric sites. Individual chiefdoms developed during this period, each generally consisting of a fortified ceremonial center, several fortified villages, and numerous farmsteads (Whayne et al. 2013: 31-38).

Most Early Mississippian period sites in the region are marked by a change in settlement pattern from relatively small village communities to dispersed farmstead systems aimed at the maximum exploitation of arable soils across the Central Mississippi Valley. Subsistence was based on intensive maize and nut agriculture but foraging also played an important role. The development of complex hierarchical chiefdoms resulted in increased social stratification. Flat topped, pyramidal temple mound construction began at some sites and continued until about A.D. 1600. In some regions, burial mounds were built and used during the earlier part of the period. Salt processing sites also show up for the first time during this period. Decorated ceramics are a hallmark of Mississippian Cultures of northeastern Arkansas and have been the basis for building and refining local and regional chronologies. The archeological investigations conducted at sites such as the Zebree site (3MS20), Mangrum site (3CG636), Rivervale site (3PO395), Priestly site (3PO490), and Moon site (3PO488) prompted a number of inquiries concerning Late-Woodland and Mississippian period sites, including exploration of Late-Woodland period nucleation, emergence of new technologies, and the development of small to large-scale villages.

 

Mississippian period phases have been defined for the region, based primarily on data from excavations at Mississippian affiliated sites. The varying levels of similarity in the Mississippian period archeological record has led to the discussion of the ways in which archeological phenomena is classified in the Central Mississippi Valley. As more data is added to the archeological record, assemblages continue to be split based upon their physical characteristics. Nevertheless, established modes of classification have provided a baseline for which particular assemblages are organized. The regional sequence during the Late Woodland/Baytown and Early Mississippian transitional periods have been subdivided into four phases: Hayti phase, the Plum Bayou phase, the Owls Bend phase, and the Big Lake phase (McNutt 1996: 130).

 

The four phases that distinguish the Late Woodland/Baytown and Early Mississippian transitional periods in northeastern Arkansas have been interpreted within the timeframe of A.D. 700-1000. Selected horizon markers for these phases include Varney’s Red Filmed ceramic vessels, bone harpoons, jars without handles, flat-based jars, Coles Creek-like ceramics, Burlington chert microliths, pans and funnels lacking effigies (McNutt 1996: 130). Characterized by excavations at the Kersey site in the Little River lowlands, the Hayti phase is defined by the predominance of red-slipped Varney ceramics and shell tempered sherds with cord-marked exteriors (McNutt 1996: 227). While there is evidence of the Plum Bayou culture stretching well into northeast Arkansas, this phase is more often recognized for diagnostics discovered at the Toltec site in Lonoke County, where flat-based shell-tempered potsherds have yielded dates to A.D. 900. These sherds are also the defining characteristic of the Owls Bend phase in Western Lowlands of northeast Arkansas. Excavations at the Watkins site (3GE346) revealed a plethora of artifacts including a single bone harpoon. Given that a number of bone harpoons were discovered at the Zebree site in Mississippi county, it was determined that Zebree and Watkins were contemporaneous. Zebree also contains a component of the Big Lake phase dating to A.D. 800/850-1050. Additionally, sites that possess a similar component are found to the south near the Lower St. Francis River (McNutt 1996: 126-127).

 

The two phases that distinguish the Early to Middle Mississippian period in northeastern Arkansas have been interpreted within the timeframe of A.D. 1000-1400. Selected horizon markers for the Cherry Valley and Lawhorn phases include Scallorn points, as well as Matthews Incised, Manly Punctated, Obion Incised, and Beakers ceramics. Among the number of ceramics that characterize this time period, some are known to contain loop/strap handles (McNutt 1996: 130). Much of what is understood regarding the Cherry Valley phase has been ascertained from the Cherry Valley site in Cross County. Archeological investigations conducted at the site revealed burial mounds built over circular charnel houses, bundle burials, and shell tempered jars with single loop handles, bottles, beaker-shaped bowls, and beakers (McNutt 1996: 234). A Middle to Late Mississippian Lawhorn component was discovered during the excavations at the Zebree site. The eight hoe chips of Tennessee Dover Chert found in context with other Mississippian period artifacts suggest a Lawhorn occupation for a period (Smith 2007: 58).

 

The Middle Mississippian (1200-1400 A.D.) fortified village of Moon in Poinsett County yielded a sizable amount of archeological data that allowed for the determination of its chronology. Three domestic structures were identified; however, due to decades of agricultural disturbance, the evidence for the structures was by no means uniform (O’Brien and Dunnell 1998: 238). In addition to the three domestic structures, the investigation revealed twenty-eight square wall trench structures, all of which were supported by posts. Copious amounts of daub and mud dauber nest fragments were discovered around the Moon structures. Many of the daub fragments displayed pole and hatch impressions. Large sections of what remained of a palisade surround the village were found, including the discovery of a second inner palisade across the southern half of the village; although, time constraints in the field disallowed for a thorough delineation. Projectile points dating from the Archaic to the Mississippian period were discovered on the ground surface, suggesting the recycling of lithic tools. This reuse pattern was also characteristic at the Priestly site in Poinsett county (O’Brien and Dunnell 1998: 232-241). Excavated features at Moon yielded 24,855 Mississippian period ceramic sherds, most of which were Mississippian Plain containing a pulverized grog and shell temper. The decorated pottery types among the coarse-shell-tempered paste Mississippi Plain vessels included: one Barton Incised var. Barton vessel, three Carson Red-on-Buff, one Mound Place var. Mound Place Incised, one Nodena Red-and-White, fourteen O’Byam var. O’Byam Incised, one Old Town Red var. Old Town, and two Rhodes Incised. The decorated pottery types among the fine-shell-tempered paste Bell Plain vessels included: two Mound Place Incised var. Mound Place vessels, one Mound Place Incised var. Chickasawba, one O’Byam Incised var. O’Byam, and Rhodes Incised (O’Brien and Dunnell 1998: 249-251).

 

The Langdon site is a fortified Middle to Late Mississippian-period occupation in southern Dunklin County, Missouri. Resting on the Malden Plain escarpment near the Little River Lowland, this large mound grouping provides further insight into Mississippian period settlements in the region. Mississippian-period Madison and Nodena projectile points, including elliptical and Banks varieties and narrowly chipped adzes/chisels were among the lithic assemblages discovered during the 1980s investigations. Ceramic assemblages include mostly Mississippi Plain sherds characterized by a grog, shell, and sand tempering. Thermoluminescence dating aided in pin pointing the chronology of the Langdon site. Of the six ceramic sherds that were analyzed during the investigation, five sherds yielded secure data. The dates from these samples ranged from A.D. 1233 ± 51 to A.D. 1690 ± 34, falling mostly within the 14th century; however, Dunnell makes a correction of the initial dates taken from the samples based on porosity correlations. The establishment of these corrections are justified by Dunnell and Feathers’ findings on routine thermoluminescence analysis and the equilibrium of radioactive decay chains. Being that disequilibrium is linked with the porosity of a sample, adjustments on the Langdon samples had to be made. This readjustment places the samples between A.D. 1100-1500 (O’Brien and Dunnell 1998: 216). Based on the typological evidence present at Langdon, the assemblages suggest an intermediary between the Cairo Lowland phase to the northeast and contemporary phases farther to the south. The fortified, condensed settlement pattern that characterizes the Langdon site provides insight into the changing settlement configurations within the Malden Plain escarpment. These changes are also seen at the Vancil site, lying further to the north. The juxtaposition of the settlement patterns within these sites as compared to the elaborate, yet diffuse Mississippian settlements preceding them, demonstrates the complex changes seen at the Langdon and Vancil sites (O’Brien and Dunnell 1998: 224). Placing these sites into a broader context, however, remains an issue.

 

The regional sequence during the Late Mississippian and Protohistoric periods are subdivided into six phases: the Nodena phase, the Parkin phase, the Greenebrier phase, the Little Red River phase, the Kent Phase, and the Belle Meade phase. These six phases that distinguish the Late Mississippian and Protohistoric periods in northeastern Arkansas have been interpreted within the timeframe of A.D. 1400-1650. Selected horizon markers include Chevron glass beads, Brass Hawk’s bell, end scrapers, rectangular shell “buttons”, Nodena points, Barton Incised ceramics, Parkin Punctated ceramics, head pots, and Catlinite disc pipes. Among the number of ceramics that characterize this time period, some are known to have applique handles (McNutt 1996: 130). While these cultures are considered somewhat contemporaneous with each other, differentiation in ceramic assemblages mark the contrast between the phases. For example, the excavations performed at various Nodena phase sites have revealed an abundance of finely tempered Bell Plain ceramics, whereas Parkin phase sites contain a majority of Mississippi Plain and Parkin Punctuated ceramics. Kent Phase sites’ assemblages contain Bell Plain to Mississippian Plain ceramics with Old Town Red, Parkin Punctated, and Barton Incised ceramics. Based on the historic record of the Hernando de Soto expedition, it is believed that the Nodena and Parkin people were visited by the 16th century Spanish conquistador; however, there is much debate as to the accuracy of de Soto’s defined “provinces” as well as his route through Arkansas (McNutt 1996: 242). In the 1500s, European contact with Native American cultural groups marked the end of the prehistoric period.
