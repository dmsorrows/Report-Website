Indian Removal and the Trail of Tears

 

In the 1780s and 1790s, contingents of Cherokee people began voluntarily migrating to Arkansas due to internal divisions and external pressures from America, Britain, France, and Spain (Cornsilk 1997; Smithers 2015: 48-49). These Cherokee settlers sought to leave their eastern homeland, separate themselves from the Cherokee Nation, and establish an independent government west of the Mississippi. Under the terms of the Treaty of 1817, the Cherokee settlers exchanged their lands in the east for equitable acreage between the Arkansas River and White River in Arkansas Territory and gained recognition as a separate nation (Cornsilk 1997; Oklahoma State Digital Library 2015). Littlefield and Parins (2011:13) noted this as the first official stage toward Indian removal to the territory. The 1820 Treaty of Doak’s Stand soon followed, by which some Choctaw first removed to lands in western Arkansas in exchange for thirteen million acres of their homeland in Mississippi. Many Cherokee settlers resided in the newly organized Arkansas territory until the Treaty of 1828 fully divested them of their lands there in exchange for seven million acres in Indian Territory (Cornsilk 1997; Oklahoma State Digital Library 2015).

 

The growing American population applied increasing pressure to the Native American peoples in the southeast and elsewhere. At the close of the first quarter of the nineteenth century, the topic of Indian removal was an issue of national concern. Presidents from Jefferson to Van Buren proposed solutions that ostensibly served to protect the United States against foreign invasion and free valuable resources for American settlers while affording native people a buffer against American expansion by removing them to the west (Littlefield and Parins 2011: xiii-xiv). Although the Arkansas Territory first appeared a suitable location for removal, by the late 1820s, population growth necessitated opening lands beyond its western border for tribal settlement (Littlefield and Parins 2011: 13).

 

President Andrew Jackson’s first annual message to Congress on December 8, 1829 clarified the administration’s position that tribes could not exercise self-governance within the bounds of states and territories. Jackson couched his argument in magnanimous language and proposed relocation as a means of averting the “weakness and decay” that befell tribes alienated from their resources. Although he stated the emigration should be voluntary, he noted that remaining within the limits of existing states and territories would subject native people to the laws of those entities where they would “ere long become merged in the mass of the population.” (Miller Center, University of Virginia 2015).

 

On April 24, 1830, the Senate voted 28 to 19 for passage of the Indian Removal Act; the House of Representatives followed on May 26, barely passing the legislation by a vote of 102 to 97. President Andrew Jackson signed the bill into law on May 28, 1830. The Act authorized the president, to cause so much of any territory belonging to the United States, west of the river Mississippi, not included in any state or organized territory, and to which the Indian title has been extinguished, as he may judge necessary, to be divided into a suitable number of districts, for the reception of such tribes or nations of Indians as may choose to exchange the lands where they now reside and remove there.

The Act extinguished Indian claims to lands in states or territories owned by the United States. The president designated an appropriation of five hundred thousand dollars to execute the provisions of the Act and provide such aid and assistance to be furnished to the emigrants as may be necessary and proper to enable them to remove to, and settle in, the country for which they may have exchanged; and also, to give them such aid and assistance as may be necessary for their support and subsistence for the first year after their removal (Library of Congress 2015).

 

The Indian Removal Act resulted in the relocation of the large southeastern tribes: the Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, and, Seminole, as well as Florida tribes (Figure 10) and tribes from the mid-west and northeast such as the Delaware, Seneca, Wyandot, Shawnee, Sauk, Fox, Potawatomi, and Miami, among others. In the 1830s, the United States signed eighty-seven tribal agreements and treaties governing removal and/or cessions of land claims; although most removals occurred from 1830 through the early 1840s, the process continued until as late as 1859 (Littlefield and Parins 2011: xiv).

 

The principal tribes associated with removal period routes through Arkansas are the Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, and Seminole. To date, there are twenty-one federally recognized tribes associated with Arkansas, many of whom bear that association because of forced removal to Indian Territory in the 1830s. The Quapaw, Osage, and Caddo had well-established ancestral ties to Arkansas. The United States facilitated the relocation and disenfranchisement of these tribes through a number of treaties unassociated with the Trail of Tears prior to the removal period of the mid-nineteenth century. The Quapaw ceded all their lands in Arkansas under the terms of the treaties of 1818 and 1824. The Treaty of 1808 (Treaty of Fort Clark) ceded all Osage lands in Arkansas north of the Arkansas River to the United States, effectively ending Osage dominion in the future territory. In 1835, the Caddo ceded their lands within the Territory of Arkansas and the United States at large (Oklahoma State Digital Library 2015). These three tribes, among others, count much of Arkansas within their current area of interest.

 

The Trail of Tears National Historic Trail (Figure 11) specifically addresses the 1838-1839 removal of the Cherokee from their homelands in Georgia, Alabama, and Tennessee to Indian Territory. The routes comprising the trail include four waterborne detachments and thirteen land detachments. Both water and land routes passed through central Arkansas in 1830 and 1839. The land routes approached from the Memphis to Little Rock Road through present-day North Little Rock. The water route travelled upstream on the Arkansas River from the confluence with the Mississippi River in route to Indian Territory (National Park Service 2016).

 

Soon after the signing of the Indian Removal Act, the 1830 Treaty of Dancing Rabbit Creek finalized the last cession of reserved Indian lands in Arkansas, resulting in the loss of the remaining Choctaw lands in Mississippi as well as reservation land in western Arkansas (Littlefield and Parins 2011: 244; Oklahoma State Digital Library 2015).

 

As a locus of removal corridors, central and western Arkansas witnessed the passage of more than 40,000 tribal people travelling to Indian Territory (Figures 12 and 13) (Paige et al. 2003: 2). It is important to note that many tribes maintain proprietary data regarding removal corridors. Therefore, the figures shown in this report is not an exhaustive account of removal routes through central and western Arkansas.

 

Geography determined the principal role central Arkansas would play in the removal of the southeastern tribes (Figures 14 and 15). The Arkansas River crossed the entirety of the state east to west from the confluence with the Mississippi. Prior to Native American removal, a system of roads was established through the Arkansas Territory stretching into Indian Territory to the west. Although the hazards faced on the roads were prominent, Native American removal parties typically preferred overland routes to water routes due to the cramped conditions endured on the riverboats. Riverboats were also known to be harbingers for numerous communicable diseases. Moreover, land routes provided removal parties with more flexibility in regard to the time and manner of removal. A network of interconnected roads extended through Arkansas during the 1830s. One of the more frequented removal routes in the southern part of Arkansas commenced from Point Chico on the Mississippi River. The route led to Ecore Fabre, present day Camden. From Ecore Fabre, the route weaved its way to the community of Washington, then to the Choctaw Nation (Littlefield and Parins 2011: 13).

 

A heavily traveled removal route in Arkansas commenced from Memphis, stretching westward across the St. Francis River and Crowley’s Ridge. Traversing north of present-day Forrest City, the route continued west, crossing the L’Anguille River. From the L’Anguille River bridge, the route weaved its way southwest to Mouth of Cache, near present day Clarendon. From this point removal parties were ferried across the White River. The route continued westerly spanning across the Grand Prairie, to its connecting point in Brownsville, then westward to present-day Jacksonville (Littlefield and Parins 2011: 13). The route connected to the Southwest Trail, turning southward to cross the bridged Bayou Meto. From Bayou Meto, the route continued into present-day North Little Rock. Additionally, this route was interconnected in Grand Prairie with another removal route leading from Arkansas Post. From Little Rock, the Memphis to Little Rock removal route connected with the Military Road in central Arkansas. The Military Road then stretched its way westward to Fort Smith. Along this route, many removal parties crossed the Arkansas River at Dardanelle, then took a route westward to either Fort Coffee, Fort Gibson, or the Choctaw Agency. In addition, the Southwest Trail connecting Little Rock and Washington joined the route leading from Point Chico (Littlefield and Parins 2011: 13).

 

A commonly used removal route in northeastern Arkansas commenced near the Southwest Trail, spanning within close proximity of Batesville. Running north of the White River, the route continued through present-day Gassville where it crossed the White River. The route continued westward through Fayetteville into the Cherokee Nation. The Missouri Road, another commonly utilized route, entered Arkansas within the vicinity of Pea Ridge. The route continued from the Pea Ridge area onto Fayetteville (Littlefield and Parins 2011: 13-14).
